Most esports org names are mostly vibes. We wanted ours to mean something specific.

The Najdorf Variation of the Sicilian Defence (1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6) is the most-analyzed opening at the grandmaster level. Bobby Fischer played it almost exclusively. Kasparov used it to beat Karpov for the world title. Magnus Carlsen still trots it out when he wants a fight. Decades of preparation, thousands of games at the top level, and the line is still not "solved." It rewards whoever studied one more move than their opponent did.

There's a useful image in there for an Overwatch team. No clever map-pool draft wins on its own. No comp beats the meta without practice. The edge comes from the work nobody sees: the VOD review at 1am, the unglamorous scrim block, the willingness to revisit a habit you thought you'd fixed.

The Najdorf also concedes the center temporarily and strikes on the wings. Anyone who has watched a tank trade space so a flanking DPS can cook on the back line knows that feeling. You give a little to take a lot.

The name itself is worth knowing. **Miguel Najdorf** was a Polish-Argentine grandmaster stranded in Buenos Aires when WWII broke out. His entire family was killed in the Holocaust. He rebuilt his life through chess, set the world simultaneous-blindfold record, and competed into his eighties. The opening that carries his name carries that story.

Esports orgs are usually named after animals, weapons, or weather. We picked an opening because we wanted the name to say something specific about how we plan to play. Stay patient, prepare deeper than the opponent, then take the sharpest line on the board.
